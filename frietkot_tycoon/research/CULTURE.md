# Frietkot Tycoon — Belgian Frietkot / Frituur Culture Research

Research dossier for tone, worldbuilding, and 1990s authenticity. Written to be
factual first, but warm and a little funny — because that is the frietkot's whole
personality. Sources cited inline as `[Source](url)`.

> **One-line pitch of the vibe:** a battered aluminium caravan on a village square,
> a fryer bubbling with beef fat, a fridge full of a dozen sauces, four white plastic
> garden chairs, and — at 2 a.m. — the single most democratic room in Belgium, where
> the mayor, three drunk students, a night-shift nurse and a lost tourist all queue
> for the same *pak friet*.

---

## 1. The frietkot as a social institution

### What it actually is

The **frietkot** (Flanders) / **friterie** or **fritkot** (Wallonia & Brussels) is a
small, usually independent stand selling fresh-cut fries fried in beef fat plus a
menu of deep-fried snacks and sauces. Belgium has roughly **4,500–5,000 of them**
nationwide — one of the highest densities of fry stands on Earth — and in a country
famously divided by language, the frietkot is repeatedly described as **one of the few
institutions that unites everyone** [Brussels Times](https://www.brusselstimes.com/1667605/fries-with-that-how-chips-became-a-staple-of-belgian-identity) [Euronews](https://www.euronews.com/2014/12/05/belgian-fries-bid-for-unesco-world-heritage-status).

Both the Flemish and the French-speaking communities have formally declared
**frietkot/friterie culture part of their intangible cultural heritage** (Flanders and
the French Community recognised it in 2014–2017), and it is inventoried in Brussels
too [Patrimoine Brussels](https://patrimoine.brussels/decouvrir/inventaires-du-patrimoine-bruxellois/inventaire-du-patrimoine-culturel-immateriel/la-culture-fritkot) [Navefri-Unafri](http://www.navefri-unafri.be/en/the-belgian-frietkot-culture/what-is-the-belgian-frietkot-culture). The
national heritage listing describes it as "a social practice based on a set of
knowledge, practices and traditions related to the production, treatment, preparation,
service and consumption of fresh *frites*" — i.e. the government officially decided
that hanging around a chip van is culture. Which it is.

### The two body types: the barrack vs. the corner shop

There are two archetypal forms, and a game should let players build/upgrade between
them:

1. **The roadside caravan / barrack (*baraque à frites*, *frietkraam*, *frietkot* in
   the literal sense — "*kot*" = shack/shed).** A fixed or semi-fixed aluminium/wooden
   box, often ex-caravan, parked on a **town square, a car park, beside a church,
   outside a railway station, at a crossroads, or at a fairground**. It has a serving
   counter, a canopy/awning to keep the rain off the queue, a hand-painted or backlit
   sign, and a few white plastic garden chairs scattered outside. This is the
   nostalgic, working-class, "real" form [The Square Gent](https://thesquare.gent/leisure/food-drink/belgian-fries-ghent-frituur/).

2. **The *frituur* proper — a fixed corner shop.** A small storefront, sometimes with
   an indoor eating area (a few high tables, sometimes an upstairs), tiled or
   stainless-steel interior, brighter lighting. Historically **the friturist often
   lived above the shop** [Frieten blog](https://frieten.blogspot.com/p/frituur-friterie-fritkot.html). This is the more "respectable/hygienic" upgrade of
   the caravan, but the food and the sauces are identical.

### Where they sit

Village square, church square, in front of the station, edge of a football pitch,
outside the school, at the entrance to a market, at the *kermis* (funfair), on a
provincial road between two towns. The location is half the business: a good spot on
the *Grote Markt* or by the last bus stop is worth a fortune. This is a natural core
mechanic for a tycoon game (map placement, footfall, competition for prime squares).

### Opening hours & the late-night crowd

Most stay **open late and all weekend**, functioning as a social hub across every
class and background [Navefri-Unafri](http://www.navefri-unafri.be/en/the-belgian-frietkot-culture/what-is-the-belgian-frietkot-culture). Typical Belgian frituur hours skew toward
**lunch, late-afternoon/dinner, and deep into the night** — many close one or two
weekdays ("*rustdag*") but blaze until the small hours Thursday–Saturday. Some famous
city spots stay open **until 5, 6, even 7 a.m.** to catch the post-club wave [TableJourney](https://tablejourney.com/belgium/bruges/late-night/).

### "Na de fuif" — the after-party tradition

The single most important cultural beat for tone: **friet na de fuif** ("fries after
the party"). After a night of dancing and *pintjes* (beers), the frituur is the
mandatory last stop before bed. It is Belgium's national hangover-prevention ritual and
its great social leveller — the drunk-but-happy crowd, the greasy paper cone, the
walk home eating with a tiny plastic fork [Brussels Express](https://tablejourney.com/belgium/bruges/late-night/) [The Square Gent](https://thesquare.gent/leisure/food-drink/belgian-fries-ghent-frituur/).
In a "deeply divided country, the humble fritkot is one of the only places that brings
people together" — never more literally true than at 3 a.m.

## 2. Terminology & slang (in-game glossary)

The name of the shop **itself is a regional identity marker** — this is gold for
flavour text.

| Region | Word for the shop | Word for the fries | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Flanders (Dutch) | **frituur**, **frietkot**, **frietkraam** | **friet / frieten / frietjes / friet** | "*kot*" = shack. "*frietjes*" (diminutive) is the everyday warm term. |
| Wallonia (French) | **friterie**, **fritkot**, older **friture / frîtûre** | **frites / une frite** | "*friterie*" is the formal French; "*fritkot*" is the beloved everyday word. |
| Brussels (bilingual) | **fritkot** (spans both) + **friterie** | frites / frieten | Signage often bilingual; "*fritkot*" originated in **Brussels dialect** from *friet* + *kot* and spread, partly displacing Flemish *frituur* and Walloon *frîtûre* [Wikipedia: Friterie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friterie). |
| Netherlands (contrast, NOT Belgium) | snackbar / cafetaria | **patat** (north) / **friet** (south) | Belgians say *friet(jes)*; northern Dutch say *patat*. Using "patat" in a Belgian setting reads as foreign [Always Talkin Food](https://alwaystalkinfood.substack.com/p/frietje-patatje-frieten-of-patat). |

**The person behind the counter:**
- **frituuruitbater / friturist / frietbakker** (Dutch) — the fry-cook / operator.
- **fritier / friturier** (French).

**Ordering — the phrasebook players should hear:**
- **"Een pak friet"** — "a packet/portion of fries" (the standard order). Also **"een
  frietje"**.
- **"Een grote / een kleine"** — a large / a small. Sizes run **klein–middel–groot**
  (small–medium–large) [Mezzoguild](https://www.mezzoguild.com/learn/dutch/vocabulary/snacks/).
- **"Een grote met"** — "a large *with*" — i.e. **with mayonnaise**, the default.
  "**Met**" (with) almost always means *met mayonaise*. In French: **"une grande avec"**
  / **"frites sauce"**.
- **"Een pak friet met stoofvlees(saus)"** — fries with beef-stew sauce, a classic.
- **"Een puntzak"** — a **paper cone** of fries (the iconic serving; increasingly
  cardboard boxes today, but the cone is the nostalgic image) [Mezzoguild](https://www.mezzoguild.com/learn/dutch/vocabulary/snacks/).
- **"Met een vorkske"** — "with a little fork" (the tiny plastic/wooden fork).

**Fries & style:**
- **Vlaamse friet** — "Flemish fries," a badge of authenticity (hand-cut, twice-fried
  in beef fat), advertised even inside Belgium [Always Talkin Food](https://alwaystalkinfood.substack.com/p/frietje-patatje-frieten-of-patat).
- **ossewit / rundvet / blanc de bœuf** — **beef tallow**, the sacred frying fat.
  Real Belgian fries are fried twice in it [Frietje Thuis](https://frietjethuis.com/en/perfect-belgian-fries-ossewit) [Golantec](https://golantec.be/Belgische%20frieten.html).

**Other useful everyday words:** *pintje* (small beer), *fuif* (party), *kermis*
(funfair), *rustdag* (closing day), *'t stationsplein* (the station square), *de Grote
Markt / la Grand-Place* (the main square).

## 3. Regional identity: Flanders vs Wallonia vs Brussels

Belgium is three worlds stitched together:

- **Flanders (north):** Dutch-speaking (Flemish). By the end of the 1980s Flanders had
  built a **considerable economic lead** over Wallonia, widening through the 1990s
  [Brussels Times](https://www.brusselstimes.com/opinion/45009/how-economics-explains-belgium-s-rifts). Culturally: tidy, entrepreneurial, self-deprecating, football-mad
  (Club Brugge, Anderlecht, Gent).
- **Wallonia (south):** French-speaking, historically the old **industrial/coal-and-steel
  heartland** now post-industrial; proud working-class identity (Standard Liège,
  Charleroi) [Brussels Times](https://www.brusselstimes.com/1258227/supply-vs-demand-how-do-voters-political-views-vary-in-flanders-and-wallonia). Home of the *mitraillette* and *fricadelle*.
- **Brussels:** officially **bilingual**, cosmopolitan, the capital, a city apart —
  where the *fritkot* word itself was born and where signs go up in both languages.

**The rivalry (keep it light & comedic).** The Flemish–Walloon friction is real and
old — rooted in **language politics** (Belgium was founded French-only; the Flemish
movement fought for Dutch) and later in **economics** (who subsidises whom) [Brussels Times](https://www.brusselstimes.com/49791/the-flemish-movement-how-language-shaped-belgium) [Language Conflict](https://www.languageconflict.org/conflict/walloon-and-flemish-in-belgium/).
For a comedy game, play it as **bickering siblings, not war**: friendly needling about
who has the better fries, whose sauce is "wrong," whose dialect is unintelligible, and
mock-outrage football derbies. Notably, research suggests the famous stereotypes are
**more about which party people vote for than how they actually think** — the two sides
are less different than the jokes imply [Psychologica Belgica](https://psychologicabelgica.com/articles/10.5334/pb.336). The comedic
truth: **everyone agrees the fries are the best thing about the whole country**, and
that's the joke — the frietkot is where the cold war goes to melt.

Great running gags: a Flemish uitbater and a Walloon friturier who insist their region
invented fries; a Brussels shop that refuses to take sides and just prints everything
twice; language-of-the-menu-board disputes; a customer who orders in the "wrong"
language and gets served anyway because *friet is friet*.

## 4. 1990s Belgium — the opening era

The game starts in the 1990s, before the euro. Details that make it feel authentic:

### Money — the Belgian Franc (BEF)

- Belgium used the **Belgian franc (BEF / "frank" / "BF")** until the euro. The
  conversion was **irrevocably fixed at 1 EUR = 40.3399 BEF** on 1 January 1999 (euro
  cash arrived in 2002) [Wikipedia: Belgian franc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belgian_franc). So **≈40.34 BEF = €1** — a handy mental
  rule for the game is "**40 francs ≈ 1 euro**."
- Coins: 50 centimes, 1, 5, 20, 50 francs. Notes: 100, 500, 1000, and larger
  (a 5,000-franc note was in circulation) [Quora](https://www.quora.com/What-was-Belgium-like-in-the-1980s).
- Everyday anchor prices (early 90s): a **loaf of bread ~54 BEF** [Quora](https://www.quora.com/What-was-Belgium-like-in-the-1980s).

### What a *pak friet* cost

Exact 90s menu prices are poorly archived online, but the shape is clear: fry prices in
Belgian frituren have **risen more than 300% since 1976**, and a modern small portion
sits around **€2–3.50** (as of 2022) [PotatoPro](https://www.potatopro.com/news/2011/belgie-de-prijs-van-friet-blijft-stijgen) [VRT NWS](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2022/10/22/frietprijzen/). Working backward, a **1990s
small *pak friet* landed around 40–60 BEF (~€1–1.5), a large around 60–80 BEF, plus
~10–20 BEF for a sauce and ~40–60 BEF for a snack** — a full "*grote met* + frikandel"
was comfortably under 100 francs. **Treat these as period-flavoured game prices, not
audited history**, but the "round-number francs, everything under 100" feel is right.
(Design tip: pricing in crunchy franc numbers — *40 fr., 55 fr., 70 fr.* — is far more
evocative than euros for the opening era.)

### Music

The 90s Belgian soundtrack is unusually iconic:
- **New Beat** — a slowed-down, hypnotic dance style **born in Antwerp/Brussels clubs**
  in the late 80s, feeding into early 90s techno; Belgium briefly "went techno"
  1990–92 [Melody Mind](https://melody-mind.de/en/knowledge/belgian/) [New Beat/Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_beat).
- **Technotronic** — *Pump Up the Jam* (1989) hit **No. 2 on the US Billboard Hot 100**,
  the sound of the era [Technotronic/Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Technotronic).
- **Front 242** — Belgian pioneers of **EBM (Electronic Body Music)**, dark and
  industrial, internationally influential [Melody Mind](https://melody-mind.de/en/knowledge/belgian/).
- **Helmut Lotti** — the crossover classical-pop tenor, 13M+ albums; **K3** (Flemish
  girl-pop trio) arrives late-90s and becomes a childhood staple [Grokipedia: Belgian artists](https://grokipedia.com/page/List_of_best-selling_Belgian_music_artists).
- Music TV: **TMF (The Music Factory)** and **MCM Belgium** aired the videos and
  countdowns [Melody Mind](https://melody-mind.de/en/knowledge/belgian/).

### TV — the nostalgia engine

- **F.C. De Kampioenen** (1990–2011) — a **massively popular Flemish sitcom** about a
  hapless amateur football club, built on stereotypical characters and farce, action
  centred on the **club canteen before/after matches**. One of the most successful
  Belgian TV programmes ever [Wikipedia: F.C. De Kampioenen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/F.C._De_Kampioenen). Its whole world — village football,
  beer, gossip — is basically frietkot-adjacent.
- **Samson en Gert** (1990–2005) — the beloved kids' show (talking dog + owner); pure
  Flemish childhood nostalgia, with crossovers into *F.C. De Kampioenen* [TV Tropes](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Series/SamsonEnGert).
- **VTM** (commercial) launched 1989 and shook up the old public-broadcaster (BRT/VRT)
  monopoly — the 90s were the birth of Flemish commercial TV.

### Cars & everyday texture

Belgian roads in the 90s: lots of **VW Golf, Opel Kadett/Astra, Ford Escort, Renault
5/Clio, Citroën, small Peugeots** — practical hatchbacks, not flash. Wealth was mainly
shown through **your house and your car** [Quora](https://www.quora.com/What-was-Belgium-like-in-the-1980s). Everyday life: brick row-houses,
Sunday visits to family, the *kermis* (funfair) rolling into town, football on the
weekend, a *pintje* on a café terrace, and the frituur on Friday night.

## 5. National pride: "the fries are ours, not French"

This is a central emotional theme and a rich comedy vein.

- **Belgian origin folklore:** the tale that villagers near the **River Meuse** fried
  potatoes in the harsh winter of **~1680** when the frozen river left no small fish to
  fry — so they cut potatoes into fish shapes and fried those instead [Twisted Food](https://twistedfood.co.uk/articles/france-and-belgium-are-fighting-over-who-invented-fries).
  Historians more soberly trace **itinerant fry stands to the Liège fair, ~1838**, with
  a showman **Frédéric "Fritz" Krieger** and family spreading fries across Wallonia and
  Flanders [Navefri-Unafri](http://www.navefri-unafri.be/en/the-belgian-frietkot-culture/what-is-the-belgian-frietkot-culture) [Home Frit' Home](https://www.homefrithome.com/en/news/frites-news/history-of-french-belgian-fries).
- **Why "French" fries?** Belgium's favourite explanations: (a) **American/British WWI
  soldiers** in French-speaking Wallonia assumed the fries (and the language) were
  French and took the name home; (b) in older English, to "**french**" a vegetable
  meant to **cut it lengthwise** — so "frenched potatoes," nothing to do with France
  [Twisted Food](https://twistedfood.co.uk/articles/france-and-belgium-are-fighting-over-who-invented-fries) [Expatica](https://www.expatica.com/be/lifestyle/food-drink/belgian-fries-101986/). Also cited: **Thomas Jefferson** brought back "potatoes served
  in the French manner" from Paris, seeding the American name [Twisted Food](https://twistedfood.co.uk/articles/france-and-belgium-are-fighting-over-who-invented-fries).
- **The heritage campaign:** In **2016**, Belgium's three language communities filed a
  joint bid to get **fritkot culture recognised by UNESCO**; it was recognised as
  **national intangible heritage (2014–2017)** first, and Belgium's fry union
  (Navefri/Unafri) kept pushing for the international list [Euronews](https://www.euronews.com/2014/12/05/belgian-fries-bid-for-unesco-world-heritage-status) [Vice](https://www.vice.com/en/article/belgians-want-their-fries-and-mayo-to-be-enshrined-in-the-unesco-heritage-list/) [Brussels Express](https://brussels-express.eu/belgian-fries-declared-unesco-cultural-heritage/).
  (Note for accuracy: online headlines sometimes overstate a completed "UNESCO win";
  the safe, factual framing is **"recognised national heritage + a long-running UNESCO
  campaign / national pride crusade."**)

**Comedy framing:** the "friet is van ons, niet van de Fransen!" ("the fries are ours,
not the French's!") outrage is deeply, sincerely felt and also very funny — a whole
nation of chill people who become passionately nationalistic about a fried potato. Perfect
for indignant NPC monologues, protest events, a "Save Our Fries" petition mini-quest,
and a rivalry with a French food-truck.

## 6. Frietkot etiquette, decor & the menu

### Decor & atmosphere

Picture: a **counter under a canopy**, a hot fryer visible behind glass, a **big
backlit or hand-painted sign** ("FRITUUR" + shop name), a **fridge/display of a dozen
sauces**, a **cold case of raw snacks** (sausages, croquettes, burgers) waiting to be
dropped in the fryer, **stacks of paper cones and cardboard trays**, ketchup-red and
mayo-white as the house colours, maybe a radio playing, and outside a **few white
plastic garden chairs and a wobbly high table**. Simple, functional, warm — the whole
country's living room [The Square Gent](https://thesquare.gent/leisure/food-drink/belgian-fries-ghent-frituur/) [Frieten blog](https://frieten.blogspot.com/p/frituur-friterie-fritkot.html).

### The unwritten etiquette

- **Mayo is default, and it's real mayo** — asking for ketchup as the main sauce is
  mildly heretical to purists (they'll serve it, but they'll judge you a little).
- **You queue, you order in shorthand** ("*een grote met*"), you don't overthink it.
- **Cash culture**, especially in the 90s — small bills and coins, exact change appreciated.
- **Eat with the little fork, walk while eating, share nothing and everything.**
- The friturist may **know the regulars by order** and start the fries before they reach
  the counter.

### The menu — sauces

Belgian sauce selection is a point of pride; a good frituur has **a dozen or more**.
Core lineup [Wikipedia: Belgian cuisine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belgian_cuisine) [The Square Gent](https://thesquare.gent/leisure/food-drink/belgian-fries-ghent-frituur/):

- **Mayonaise** (the default), **ketchup**, **curry ketchup**
- **Andalouse** (mayo + tomato + peppers) — hugely popular
- **Samurai** (mayo + Tunisian/harissa chilli — spicy) — the fashionable hot one
- **Sauce riche** (pink, tartar-based), **tartare** (mayo + gherkins)
- **Mammoet / mammouth** (mayo, tomato, onion, garlic, soy)
- **Pepersaus** (pepper), **stoofvleessaus** (dark beef-stew gravy — a Belgian favourite
  on fries), **joppiesaus**
- **Zigeunersaus** (paprika/pepper, borrowed from Germany), **bicky dressing** (for the
  Bicky burger)
- **Looksaus** (garlic mayo), **cocktail**, **américaine**

### The menu — snacks (fried, ordered alongside fries)

Belgium's beloved *frituursnacks*, roughly by popularity [Unipage survey](https://unipage.be/en/blog/what-are-the-most-popular-frituur-snacks-survey-results) [Wikipedia: Belgian cuisine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belgian_cuisine):

- **Boulet / frikadel (meatball)** — often ranked #1, esp. with stew sauce.
- **Mexicano** — spicy elongated meat patty; a cult favourite.
- **Frikandel** — the skinless minced-meat sausage; **"frikandel speciaal"** = split,
  filled with mayo, ketchup & chopped onions. (Note: in Wallonia/France **"fricadelle"**
  usually = this same skinless sausage; regional naming varies.)
- **Bicky Burger** — an iconic Belgian fast-food burger with its own dressing;
  variants Bicky Cheese / Bicky Fish.
- **Viandel**, **cervela / cervelas** (a scored sausage that curls when fried),
  **curryworst**.
- **Kroketten** — **kaaskroket** (cheese) and **garnaalkroket** (grey-shrimp) croquettes;
  **vleeskroket / bitterballen**.
- **Loempia** (spring roll), **kipnuggets / chicken fingers**, **kippenboutje**
  (drumstick), **brochette**, **sate**, **ragoutzakje**.
- **Stoofvlees / carbonade** — Flemish beer-braised beef stew, served **with** fries.

### The signature sandwich — the *mitraillette*

The **mitraillette** ("submachine gun") — a Brussels/Wallonia icon: **half a baguette
stuffed with fried meat (sausage/burger/steak) + a full portion of fries + sauce**
(often samurai), sometimes salad and cheese. Also called **"Américain"** in French-speaking
areas and northern France. Beloved by **students** for being enormous and cheap; the
baguette is the "barrel," the hot fries the "bullets" [Wikipedia: Mitraillette](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mitraillette) [Saveur](https://www.saveur.com/brussels-sandwich-mitraillette/).

## 7. Customer archetypes (the human comedy)

The frituur is a great equaliser — its whole charm is who stands in the same queue.
Cast:

- **The Regular (*de vaste klant*)** — same order daily, knows the friturist, complains
  fondly about the weather/football/politics. Backbone of the business.
- **The Students** — loud, broke, hungry, split a mitraillette three ways, order the
  spicy snack to look tough, pay in coins.
- **The Workers** — bricklayers, delivery drivers, night-shift nurses; big portion, fast,
  a boulet with stew sauce, back to the van.
- **The Family** — Friday-night "we're not cooking tonight" ritual; kids get frikandel,
  parents get a shared *grote*, everyone fights over the sauces.
- **The Drunk Night Crowd (*na de fuif*)** — 2–4 a.m., blissful, generous, occasionally
  chaotic; orders everything; the soul of the late shift.
- **The Tourist** — asks if the fries are French (danger), photographs the cone,
  over-tips, mispronounces "frituur."
- **The Purist / Foodie** — insists on beef-fat frying and real mayo, sniffs at ketchup,
  will lecture you about double-frying and ossewit.
- **The Football Crowd** — before/after the match, tribal scarves, in a hurry, in a mood.
- **The Local Character / Village Eccentric** — every square has one; unofficial mascot.

## 8. Belgian humour & tone to match

Belgian comedy (esp. Flemish) is **dry, self-deprecating, absurdist, and fond of the
small and the local** — see the everyman farce of **F.C. De Kampioenen** (village
losers you love) and the gentle silliness of **Samson en Gert** [Wikipedia: F.C. De Kampioenen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/F.C._De_Kampioenen) [TV Tropes](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Series/SamsonEnGert).
The register is **understated, never grandiose**; Belgians mock their own dysfunction
(the endless government-formation jokes, the language wars, the weather) with affection.
Surrealism is a national trait (Magritte's country). Warmth beats snark. The comedy of
Frietkot Tycoon should be: **cosy, wry, proud-of-small-things, and quietly obsessed with
fried potatoes** — big feelings about tiny stakes.

## Game design hooks

### Customer archetypes → preferences (mechanics)

| Archetype | Orders | Wants | Tips / patience | Flavour |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **De Vaste Klant** (Regular) | "the usual" (small *met*) | speed, being remembered, chat | loyal, low tip, high patience | Bonus rep if you start their fries before they order |
| **Students** | mitraillette, spicy snacks (mexicano/samurai) | quantity for cheap | low tip, medium patience | Come in packs; boost sales but haggle |
| **Workers** | boulet + stoofvleessaus, large fries | fast, filling, hot | medium tip, LOW patience (on a break) | Rush-hour lunch spike |
| **Family** | 1 large shared + kids' frikandels | variety of sauces, seating | medium tip, medium patience | Friday-night bonus; want plastic chairs/tables |
| **Night Crowd (na de fuif)** | everything, extra sauce | anything, NOW, more salt | high tip, low judgement | 2–4 a.m. surge; messy; huge revenue |
| **Tourist** | small *met* + photo | novelty, English menu | high tip, high patience | Asks "are these French?" — dialogue mini-event |
| **Purist/Foodie** | fries only, real mayo | beef-fat frying, double-fry, quality | tips well IF quality high, else complains | Rep multiplier for authenticity upgrades |
| **Football Crowd** | quick snack + fries | speed pre/post-match | medium tip, LOW patience | Event spike on match days; mood swings on result |

### Tone / writing guidelines

1. **Cosy > cynical.** Warm, understated, self-deprecating. Belgians mock themselves
   with affection, never contempt. Small stakes, big feelings.
2. **The fries are sacred — and that's the joke.** Sincere passion about a fried potato.
   NPCs get genuinely emotional about mayo, beef fat, and "French" slander.
3. **Keep the language war light.** Flemish vs Walloon = bickering siblings, playful
   stereotypes, football derbies — never bitterness. Brussels shrugs and prints the menu
   twice.
4. **Use the real vocabulary as texture:** *een grote met, puntzak, na de fuif, pintje,
   rustdag, stoofvleessaus, ossewit.* Sprinkle, don't drown.
5. **Local and specific beats generic.** Name the square, the sauce, the football club,
   the weather. Absurd-but-mundane (very Belgian / very Magritte).
6. **Franc-era flavour:** round franc prices, cash, the coming euro, boxy 90s cars,
   music-TV in the corner. Nostalgia is the secret sauce.
7. **Everyone is welcome at the counter.** The core emotional truth to protect: the
   frietkot is the one place the whole divided country stands in the same queue, at
   3 a.m., agreeing on exactly one thing.
